### both

Refers to two items or options together, indicating that each of the two is included or true.

_Both John and Mary are coming to the party._ (John and Mary are both coming.)
_I like both apples and oranges._ (I like apples and oranges equally.)

### either

Refers to one of two items or options, but not both. It implies that only one of the two is possible or true.

_You can choose either tea or coffee._ (You can have tea or coffee, but not both.)  
_Either path will take you to the beach._ (Both paths lead to the beach, so either one is fine.)  

In this case, since both paths are valid choices, it might feel like "either" is referring to both. However,
grammatically, "either" is still about picking one option at a time. You can choose either path, and you'll reach the
beach, but it does not mean both paths simultaneously—it means one or the other.

### neither

Refers to none of the two items or options. It indicates that not one and not the other is true.

_Neither John nor Mary is coming to the party._ (John is not coming, and Mary is not coming either.)  
_Neither option is good for me._ (Both options are bad.)
