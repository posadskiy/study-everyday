### fairly

Quite usually means 'fairly' or 'a medium amount'

_I feel quite hungry now._  
_Repairing the machine is quite difficult._  
_The talk was quite interesting._  
_We were quite surprised at the result._

**If we stress quite,** the meaning is negative.

_The exhibition was quite good, but I've seen better ones._  
_I get up quite early, but not as early as you do._

**If we stress the adjective,** the meaning is positive (but not as positive as very).

_The exhibition was quite good. I enjoyed looking round it._   
_I got up quite early. I had a lot of jobs to do._

### favourable comment

When we make a favourable comment, we usually say quite, not rather.

_The book was quite interesting._  
_It's quite warm now._  
_It was quite nice walking through the park._

### unfavourable comments

In unfavourable comments, we usually say rather, but quite is possible.

_The book was rather boring/quite boring._
_It was rather awkward/quite awkward taking my suitcase on the underground._

### to a surprising or unusual degree

Rather in a favourable comment means 'to a surprising or unusual degree'.

_It's rather warm for October._ (It isn't usually so warm.)  
_I didn’t know David can cook. He’s rather good at it._  
_I expect Tom's jokes were awful. ~ Actually they were rather funny._

We can use rather with a comparative but not quite.

_The meal took rather longer than we expected._

### completely / totally

With next adjectives, quite means 'completely' or 'totally'.

`absurd, alone, amazing, awful, brilliant, certain, dead, different, dreadful, extraordinary, false, hopeless, horrible,
impossible, perfect, ridiculous, right, sure, true, useless, wrong`

_What you said is quite wrong._ (= completely wrong)  
_The idea is quite absurd._ (= totally absurd)  
_The situation is quite hopeless._
